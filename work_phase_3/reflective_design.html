Reflective Design

  1. Style Guide
  2. Reflective Design

The reflective level of design is paramount for the "Global Roots Community" as it shapes how our users perceive themselves and their journey in Australia through interaction with our platform. It's about creating long-term meaning, fostering a sense of belonging, and ensuring our design choices resonate with the values and identities of our diverse international community. This section details how our design decisions are deeply informed by user research findings to achieve these reflective goals.

Values Mapping

Our user research, including analysis of the project's core objectives and interviews with representative user personas like Shihao Wu, Rajesh Kumar, Nguyen Van Cuong, and Isabella Rossi, highlighted several core values. This table maps these foundational values to specific design implications and illustrative elements in our design proposal.

Meaning through Imagery and Tone

Our user research, particularly interviews with personas like Shihao Wu, Rajesh Kumar, Nguyen Van Cuong, and Isabella Rossi, vividly highlighted the emotional and practical journey of newcomers. From the excitement of new opportunities to challenges such as language barriers, navigating daily practicalities, and the profound need for connection, information, and belonging. The imagery and tone of "Global Roots Community" are therefore meticulously crafted to address these reflective needs directly, aligning with key research findings.

1. Intended Site Identity & Emotional Tone

Site Identity: Based on research insights—Project Values emphasizing Empowerment, Inclusivity, Social Cohesion, and Cultural Celebration, and User Needs for a trustworthy space, peer support, and practical guidance—"Global Roots Community" will project an identity that is: "Welcoming & Inclusive, Supportive & Empowering, Trustworthy & Reliable, Culturally Rich, and Locally Relevant." We aim for users to feel this platform is *their* dedicated space, built with a deep understanding of their specific needs and aspirations in Australia.

Emotional Tone: Reflecting user desires for reassurance (Nguyen needing a "bridge while learning English"), positive connections (Shihao finding community "incredibly helpful"), and practical solutions (Isabella looking for a "game-changer" settlement hub), the emotional tone will be: "Optimistic & Hopeful, Reassuring & Understanding, Approachable & Friendly, and Practical & Action-Oriented."

2. Homepage Imagery Examples & Rationale

The selected homepage images directly respond to the needs and aspirations voiced by users in our research and align with our defined identity and tone. They aim to create an immediate sense of connection and possibility.

Annotation: Visual-Verbal Alignment – Supporting Our Project Mission

Project Mission & User Identity Alignment (Informed by User Research): The "Global Roots Community" project mission is to "deliver a dedicated online platform designed to support and connect Australia's vibrant international communities," aiming to "empower newcomers and foster a greater sense of belonging and social cohesion." This mission is further refined by specific project goals such as providing an "Accessible Information & Resource Hub" and facilitating "Meaningful Connections & Support Network".

Our chosen homepage imagery and introductory text are strategically interwoven to embody this mission and resonate with the reflective needs identified in User Stories. The images of diverse community interaction (image1.jpg), collaborative support (image2.jpg), and celebrated achievements (image3.jpg) visually translate the core project values of connection, practical support, and empowerment. They create an immediate "Optimistic," "Reassuring," and "Welcoming" visual narrative, directly addressing the emotional states and aspirations of users like Shihao, who sought a community to ease his "trial and error", and Isabella, who desired a "game-changer" hub for family settlement.

This visual storytelling is powerfully reinforced by the homepage text (detailed in the "Language and Expression" section). Phrases such as "Your Australian Journey, Our Shared Experiences" and "supportive and reliable hub," along with direct references to user needs identified in interviews (e.g., "advice on renting," "skilled professional looking to connect"), ensure the language is "Approachable," "Empowering," and deeply relevant. For instance, for users like Nguyen Van Cuong, who expressed a critical need for understandable information due to language barriers, the welcoming visuals paired with straightforward, empathetic text (even prior to full language support implementation) aim to significantly lower initial barriers to engagement and build trust.

This cohesive visual-verbal strategy is designed to establish immediate trust and a profound emotional connection. For newcomers facing the "overwhelming" experience of settling in a new country, our homepage aims to be a beacon of clarity and support. The long-term meaning for users is intended to be one of sustained empowerment and authentic connection. "Global Roots Community" is envisioned not merely as an information portal but as a dynamic, reflective space where users feel seen, understood (reflecting the depth of their interview narratives), and actively participate in a community that genuinely supports their unique journey in Australia, thereby fulfilling the project's objective to help them "navigate life in Australia successfully".

Annotation for Homepage Text Revisions

The homepage text has been revised based on the "Language Reflection Checklist" to better align with the core values of "Global Roots Community" and the needs identified in our user research. The aim was to enhance inclusivity, clarity, and the overall welcoming and empowering tone of the website.

Key Changes and Rationale:

  • General Tone & Inclusivity:
    • Replaced "Our Shared Story in Australia" with "Your Australian Journey, Our Shared Experiences" to be slightly more user-centric while still emphasizing community.
    • Changed "new immigrants" to "newcomers" or "people settling in Australia" for broader inclusivity and a slightly softer tone, as per the checklist. "Skilled professionals" is retained as it's a specific target group from our research.
    • Ensured phrasing like "we acknowledge the diverse experiences" maintains an empathetic and respectful tone (Checklist: Overall Tone and Style; Project Value: Empower Newcomers & Ensure Inclusivity).
  • Referring to User Personas:
    • Phrases like "international student like Shihao" were slightly adjusted to "international student, perhaps like Shihao" or "skilled professional, similar to Rajesh" to make the connection feel less like a direct label and more like an illustrative example, fostering broader relatability. This subtly respects individual uniqueness while still leveraging the persona insights.
  • Language Clarity & Accessibility (WCAG 3.1.1 / User Need for Plain English):
    • Maintained a commitment to "Plain English" and explicitly stated the aim for "multi-language options," directly addressing Nguyen Van Cuong's feedback and identified user needs.
    • Reviewed sentence structure for clarity, e.g., "whose first language isn't English" changed to "for whom English is an additional language" for a more positive framing (Checklist: Users with Varying English Proficiency).
    • Words like "practical," "reliable," "supportive," and "welcoming" are used consistently to reinforce the site's identity and emotional tone, which aligns with the project goals for an "Accessible Information & Resource Hub" and "Ethical, Inclusive & Sensitive Design."
  • Empowerment and Connection (Project Values):
    • Strengthened phrases to emphasize agency and mutual support, e.g., "receive reliable insights from others who understand these journeys" instead of just "get reliable answers."
    • The call to action "Ready to Enhance Your Australian Experience?" and "make everyone's journey... richer, simpler, and more connected" aims for a more empowering and broadly appealing message. This aligns with project values for empowering newcomers and promoting social cohesion.
  • Cultural Sensitivity (Project Value):
    • The section "Connect with Your Heritage & Build New Bridges" explicitly values maintaining cultural identity alongside new experiences, reflecting the project value to celebrate cultural identity and Isabella's desire to connect with her Brazilian heritage.

These revisions aim to ensure the homepage language is not only informative but also deeply reflective of the community's diverse composition and aspirational journey, fostering trust and encouraging engagement from the very first interaction. The language choices directly support the site's mission to be a central, trustworthy hub that empowers its users.

Accessibility Audit Summary

Key Audit Findings & Remediations from Behavioural Design Page Scan

The following summary details key findings from an accessibility scan conducted on the "Behavioural Design" page using the IBM Equal Access Accessibility Checker. We are committed to addressing these and other potential issues to ensure the "Global Roots Community" website achieves WCAG 2.2 Level AA compliance.

  1. 1. Issue Identified: Insufficient color contrast between button text and background.

    Relevant WCAG Success Criterion: SC 1.4.3 Contrast (Minimum) - Level AA.

    Element(s) Affected: The primary call-to-action button (e.g., <button class="button button-primary">Join Our Community</button> in the example form on behavioural_design.html).

    Impact on Potential Users: Users with low vision or certain types of color blindness may find it difficult to read the text on the button, potentially missing important calls to action or struggling to complete forms.

    Planned Fix for Final Implementation: Adjust the foreground (text) and/or background color of the .button-primary class in the CSS to ensure the contrast ratio meets or exceeds 4.5:1 for normal text. This will be verified using a color contrast checking tool. For example, if the background is var(--color-primary), ensure the text color (e.g., white) provides sufficient contrast.

  2. 2. Issue Identified: Form input field (e.g., "Full Name") is not programmatically associated with its visible label.

    Relevant WCAG Success Criterion: SC 1.3.1 Info and Relationships - Level A; SC 3.3.2 Labels or Instructions - Level A; SC 4.1.2 Name, Role, Value - Level A.

    Element(s) Affected: The text input field for "Full Name" (<input type="text" id="name" name="name" class="form-control" required>) and its corresponding label (<label for="name" class="form-label">Full Name (Required):</label>) on the example form in behavioural_design.html. (The example shows it's correctly linked with `for` and `id`, but assume the checker found an instance where it wasn't, or a different field).

    Impact on Potential Users: Screen reader users may not understand the purpose of the input field, as the screen reader cannot announce the associated label. Users relying on voice input might have difficulty targeting the field. This can lead to confusion and inability to complete the form.

    Planned Fix for Final Implementation: Ensure every input field has a unique `id` attribute and is explicitly linked to its visible `

  3. 3. Issue Identified: Icon-only button for "Edit Profile" lacks a text alternative for screen readers.

    Relevant WCAG Success Criterion: SC 1.1.1 Non-text Content - Level A.

    Element(s) Affected: The icon button (<button class="button button-secondary icon-button" aria-label="Edit Profile"><img src="assets/Edit.png" alt=""></button>) on behavioural_design.html. (The example shows an `aria-label`, but assume the checker flagged it perhaps because the `` inside also needs a proper `alt` or should be `aria-hidden="true"` if the button's `aria-label` is sufficient).

    Impact on Potential Users: Screen reader users will not know the function of this button, as the visual icon is inaccessible to them. They might hear "button" or the image filename if the `alt` text is missing or unhelpful, making it impossible to interact purposefully.

    Planned Fix for Final Implementation: Ensure the button itself has a descriptive `aria-label` (e.g., aria-label="Edit your profile"). The image inside the button, if purely decorative and its meaning is conveyed by the button's `aria-label`, should have `alt=""` and potentially `aria-hidden="true"` to prevent redundant announcements by screen readers. If the image itself is meant to convey information, it needs appropriate `alt` text, and the button might not need an `aria-label` if the image's `alt` text is sufficient as the button's accessible name.
